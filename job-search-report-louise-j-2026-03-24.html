Interview Preparation Guide

5 Likely Interview Questions & Talking Points

Q1: "Tell me about your experience supporting C-level executives. What was most challenging?"

Key Points to Cover:

  • 25+ years supporting CEOs and senior executives across multiple industries (healthcare, finance, consulting)
  • Managed multiple high-demand executives simultaneously (e.g., "At Barclays, supported 4 Managing Directors across 3 time zones")
  • Navigate different executive personalities and working styles
  • Most challenging: Competing priorities and last-minute changes. "I developed systems to manage ambiguity—always having 2-3 plan options ready, anticipating needs before executives articulate them, and maintaining calm under pressure."
  • Outcome: Built reputation as someone executives rely on for operational continuity and problem-solving

Example Story: "When I joined Gen4 Dental Partners, the CEO's calendar was fragmented across multiple systems. I consolidated everything into a unified digital workflow, reducing scheduling conflicts by 80% and freeing up the CEO to focus on strategy instead of operational friction."

Q2: "How do you handle confidential information and maintain discretion?"

Key Points to Cover:

  • Confidentiality is non-negotiable; foundational to executive trust
  • Experience handling sensitive correspondence, board materials, M&A discussions, personal matters
  • Demonstrate judgment: "I don't just keep secrets—I understand what information is relevant to whom and when. I never volunteer unnecessary details, even internally."
  • Describe a specific example of discretion in action (e.g., handling a board-level dispute, sensitive personnel issue, etc.) without breaching confidence
  • Systems: Secure document management, access controls, understanding of NDAs and legal/regulatory requirements

Example Story: "At [previous company], I coordinated a sensitive M&A transaction requiring strict confidentiality with external counsel, board members, and key stakeholders. I maintained separate communication channels, ensured documents were properly classified, and never disclosed more than necessary. The transaction closed successfully without information leakage."

Q3: "Describe your experience with remote work and managing across time zones."

Key Points to Cover:

  • Currently working fully remote at Gen4 (3+ years experience)
  • Early career in London, UK, managing EMEA/Asia coordination for international firms
  • Tools: Proficient with Google Workspace, Outlook, Slack, Zoom, Navan, Asana, etc.
  • Time zone mastery: Calendar management across 16+ time zones; scheduling strategies to accommodate all parties
  • Async communication: Documenting decisions, maintaining shared spaces (SharePoint, Slack), ensuring no executive is caught off-guard
  • Remote professionalism: Over-communication, clear documentation, regular check-ins with exec(s), proactive flag-raising

Example Story: "At Barclays, I coordinated quarterly business reviews for Managing Directors across London, NYC, and Tokyo offices. I scheduled meetings using color-coded time zone tools, prepared 24-hour briefing packets for each time zone, and maintained running agendas that accommodated async input. No executive ever felt information-starved despite the geographic spread."

Q4: "Tell me about a time you improved a process or saved the executive team time."

Key Points to Cover:

  • EAs are process improvement leaders; quantify impact when possible
  • Example process wins: Calendar consolidation, travel booking automation, meeting prep templates, document management systems, vendor consolidation, budget forecasting tools
  • Demonstrate curiosity: "I regularly audit processes to find inefficiencies. I'm not content with 'that's how we've always done it.'"
  • Stakeholder management: "I work with IT, finance, operations teams to implement tools that multiply executive effectiveness"
  • Metrics: "Reduced meeting prep time from 4 hours to 1 hour per board meeting through templates and automation"

Example Story: "At Gen4, the CEO was spending 2 hours per week manually consolidating updates from different department heads for leadership meetings. I designed a simple Slack-based system where updates flowed directly into a shared dashboard. This freed up the CEO for strategic thinking and reduced meeting prep time by 80%. It's a small thing, but multiplied across 50 weeks, that's nearly 80 hours of executive time reclaimed annually."

Q5: "How do you stay organized with competing priorities and last-minute changes?"

Key Points to Cover:

  • Acknowledge reality: "Last-minute changes are the nature of executive support. I don't fight the chaos—I architect systems to absorb it."
  • Tools & systems: Priority matrix, color-coding, recurring task automation, delegation strategies
  • Communication: "I over-communicate status. Executives always know what's on track, what's at risk, and what I need from them."
  • Example: "I maintain a running 'executive briefing' document updated daily—top 5 priorities, decisions needed, upcoming risks. This prevents last-minute scrambles."
  • Mindset: "Calm under pressure. I don't panic. I prioritize ruthlessly and communicate trade-offs transparently."

Example Story: "The CEO once called me Friday afternoon asking to reorganize the entire executive offsite (Monday) due to a major acquisition opportunity. Instead of panicking, I had a framework ready: (1) adjusted agenda; (2) new travel arrangements; (3) updated materials; (4) communicated changes to stakeholders. We delivered a world-class offsite despite the chaos. That's what I do—I'm the operational shock absorber."

Questions to Ask the Interviewer

Asking thoughtful questions signals that you're evaluating the role thoughtfully:

  • "Can you describe the executive I would support—their working style, communication preferences, and biggest operational pain points?" (Shows you want to understand the person, not just the job)
  • "What does success look like in the first 90 days, first year?" (Demonstrates commitment to impact and measurable goals)
  • "How is executive support valued here? What opportunities exist for growth into roles like Chief of Staff or Office Manager?" (Shows career ambition and commitment)
  • "What tools and systems are currently in place for calendar management, travel, meeting prep? Are there pain points you'd like to improve?" (Signals process improvement mindset)
  • "What qualities do you think define an exceptional Executive Assistant in your environment?" (Gives you insight into company culture and values)
  • "How does the executive team currently operate remotely? What communication cadence and tools are in place?" (Shows you understand remote work dynamics)

Salary Negotiation Tips

  • Research First: Use Glassdoor, Payscale, ZipRecruiter salary data for specific companies to arrive at an informed range
  • Delay Salary Talk: Never disclose your salary expectation first. If pressed, say: "I'm flexible based on the full compensation package and role scope. What's your budgeted range?"

  • Negotiate Total Comp: If base salary is below target, negotiate signing bonus, home office setup budget, professional development, flexible hours, additional PTO
  • Get It in Writing: Always request a written offer before accepting. Include: base salary, bonus structure/target, benefits, start date, remote work terms
