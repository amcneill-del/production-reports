What the Current Plan Gets Right — and What It’s Missing

The marketing plan is well-structured and correctly identifies the four core priorities: PRD touchpoint performance, lapsed referrer re-engagement, Conner associate ramp, and the Sanford expansion. The flags are accurate, the benchmarks are solid, and the 30-day milestones are realistic.

However, cross-referencing against data from the Go Grow Referral Pipeline Analysis, the PRD Touchpoint Calculator, the YoY Perio Performance Report, and current online presence research surfaces seven additional insights that should be layered into the plan before the first meeting. These range from a critical digital gap that could silently undermine every Conner introduction visit, to PRD efficiency math that reveals the real throughput ceiling Keri is hitting.

PRD Touchpoint Math Reveals the Real Ceiling Efficiency Gap

From the PRD Touchpoint Calculator and Metrics Analysis sessions

The plan states Keri is at 8 touchpoints/week against a capacity of 10 (80% utilization). That looks acceptable at face value. But the PRD Calculator analysis revealed a deeper problem across the portfolio: inflated in-person touchpoint time estimates are eating PRD hours and capping throughput.

The Core Problem

At 20 hours/week with a 75/25 in-person/virtual split, the model needs to know how long each touchpoint takes. If Keri’s in-person visits average 2.5–3+ hours (factoring drive time in the Moore County/Sanford geography), then 8 touchpoints/week may actually be at or near true capacity — not 80% of it.

Cross-portfolio insight: In the PRD Calculator analysis, we found that practices with inflated in-person time estimates (3+ hours) produced the same number of touchpoints as those with lean estimates (1.5 hours) — the extra PRD hours just got absorbed by windshield time. Michael Toms achieved the best NP-per-PRD-hour ratio (8.0) with only 4 PRD hours/week because their time-per-touchpoint was lean. Woodyard, with similar inflated drive times, returned only 4.1 NP per PRD hour — a 2x efficiency gap.

What to Add to the Plan

ActionOwnerWhen
Audit Keri’s actual time-per-touchpoint: log drive time separately from face time for 2 weeks. Determine if the 75/25 in-person/virtual split is optimal for a two-location geography. Keri / Jayme Week 1–2
Model a 50/50 in-person/virtual split. Portfolio analysis showed this can increase touchpoint throughput 40–60% with zero added hours. Southern Pines → Sanford drive time makes this especially relevant. Jayme Week 2
Track NP per PRD hour as the primary efficiency metric going forward. Current plan tracks touchpoint volume but not yield per hour invested. Keri Ongoing

Referrer Touch Frequency

At 8 touchpoints/week across 128 referring partners, Keri is touching each referrer approximately once every 16 weeks. The target from the PRD Calculator is 2–4 weeks between touches. Even with A/B/C tiering (concentrating on top 30–40 referrers), the math only works if virtual touchpoints count and are systematically deployed.

From plan context, web research, and PRD Calculator analysis

The plan correctly flags two-location complexity and says not to apply Southern Pines targets to Sanford. But it doesn’t go far enough. Sanford isn’t just a satellite — it’s a new market entry that needs its own referral development strategy.

What We Know About Sanford

  • Opened January 2026 (3 months old at time of this plan)
  • Located at 824 Spring Lane, Sanford, NC 27330
  • Listed on the Sanford Area Growth Alliance business directory
  • Website already reflects “Southern Pines & Sanford NC” in page titles
  • No separate Google Business Profile detected in search results
PRD capacity math for two locations: The PRD Calculator showed that drive time between locations is the hidden killer of touchpoint throughput. Southern Pines to Sanford is approximately 30–35 minutes each way. If Keri is splitting her 20 hours/week between two markets, she’s losing 4–5 hours/week just to windshield time between locations. That reduces her effective touchpoint capacity from 10/week to approximately 7–8 — which is exactly where she’s landing.

What to Add to the Plan

ActionOwnerWhen
Confirm whether Sanford has its own Google Business Profile (separate from Southern Pines). If not, create one immediately — Sanford-area GPs searching for a local periodontist won’t find the practice without it. Marketing / Web Week 1
Batch Sanford touchpoints on dedicated days (e.g., Tues/Thurs = Sanford, Mon/Wed/Fri = Southern Pines) to eliminate round-trip drive time waste. Keri Week 2
Build a Sanford-specific prospect list: identify all GP, general dentist, and family dentistry offices within 15 miles of 824 Spring Lane. These are net-new targets not in the current 128 RL list. Keri / Jayme Week 2–3
Position Conner as the Sanford provider. If he’s seeing patients there, lead all Sanford prospecting with Conner — it gives him a “home market” to own and separates his ramp from Van Scoyoc’s established Southern Pines base. Practice Leadership Strategic decision

Review Themes That Matter for Referrals

Patients consistently mention: same assistant every visit (continuity of care), doctor calls patients at home after procedures (above-and-beyond service), works with your insurance (reduces friction), and friendly and reassuring demeanor. These are exactly the messages referring offices need to hear — they address the top concern GPs have when referring out: “will my patient have a good experience and come back to me?”

Untapped lever: Keri’s leave-behinds and Conner one-pager should include a curated selection of review quotes. A printed card with 3–4 patient quotes (especially ones mentioning being called at home post-procedure, or always seeing the same assistant) does more to build referring-office confidence than any stat sheet.

Case Acceptance Is a Portfolio-Wide Problem — Frame It Accordingly Strategic Framing

From the YoY Perio Performance Report (14 locations)

The plan flags case acceptance at 67.6% vs. 70% and avg $ accepted at $3,406 vs. $4,500. These are real gaps. But the YoY Perio Performance analysis across all 14 locations revealed that Case $ Accepted % declined from 47.0% to 38.4% portfolio-wide. This means patients across the entire network are accepting less comprehensive treatment plans.

Why this reframing matters: If case acceptance and avg $ accepted are presented to Van Scoyoc practice leadership as a local failure, it creates defensiveness. If it’s presented as a portfolio-wide trend that Van Scoyoc can lead the recovery on (especially via LANAP positioning), it creates buy-in. The Beaches marketing plan review used this same reframing approach successfully.

LANAP as the Avg $ Accepted Lever

The plan correctly identifies LANAP as the biggest production opportunity. LANAP cases typically command higher case values than standard perio or grafting. The fastest path to closing the $3,406 → $4,500 gap is not more referrals — it’s more LANAP referrals from offices that currently send only extractions and basic perio. This means Keri’s re-engagement messaging should lead with LANAP education, not just “we have a new doctor.”

What to Add to the Plan

ActionOwnerWhen
Develop a LANAP-specific educational piece for referring offices: what it is, which patients are candidates, how it differs from traditional surgery, and expected outcomes. This is the conversion tool for moving offices from “extractions-only” referrers to full-scope referrers. Dr. Van Scoyoc / Marketing Week 2–3
Tag referrals in RL by case type (LANAP vs. perio vs. grafting vs. extraction). Track whether re-engaged lapsed referrers are sending higher-value case types over time. Keri Ongoing
Frame the avg $ accepted gap as a portfolio-wide trend in the meeting with practice leadership. Position Van Scoyoc as a leader in the LANAP-driven recovery, not a laggard in the case acceptance metric. Jayme First meeting
